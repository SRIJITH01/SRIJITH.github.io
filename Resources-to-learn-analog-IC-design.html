Fundamentals

To explore fundamentals of analog design, you need have a strong foundation in circuit analysis such as KCL, KVL, thevenin equivalent, norton equivalent, bode plots.

  • Understanding how to find the poles and zeros of any circuit is very critical and necessary to get good at analog design. ZVTC method is widely used for this, Prof. Ali Hajmiri has a very good paper[1] and video on youtube (link) for this.
  • As for the building blocks of analog circuits CS, CG, CD Amplifiers, Prof. Razavi's "Design of Analog CMOS Integrated Circuits" is a good suggestion. Another good book that takes less time to go through is "Analysis and Design of Elementary MOS Amplifier Stages" by Prof. Boris Murmann
  • There are also some excellent magazines that cover many fundamental topics as well as more specific circuits that are worth going through when you need to revise for an interview:

    • Circuit Insights by Prof. Ali Sheikholeslami
    • The Analog Mind by Prof. Behzad Razavi
    • A Circuit for All Seasons by Prof. Behzad Razavi

System-level

The chip design industry is very big and it takes a life time to go through all the papers, books, videos on just data converters. A goldmine to understand tradeoffs, prior research and future directions of areas such as data converters, frequency synthesizers, wireless communication chips, wireline communication, biomedical chips, power management, memory design, RF Design is to listen to best people in the academia & industry. SSCS has many videos, short courses, tutorials exactly for this.

  • ISSCC has been organizing circuit insights session for the past few years that cover some great range of topics from intution to ASIC designs
  • SSCS has regular webinars with experts from each field every month. some of these popular webinars are available on youtube playlists organized by each field, but not all webinars are uploaded here, you can find a complete set of webinars by joining the SSCS community
  • You can also join these webinars by registering for free on the site
  • Here is a collection of short tutorials PPTs from ISSCC at this site

Competitions to tapeouts

A tapeout early in career can be a big boost, but its very hard to find an opportunity to tapeout. One way is to join a research lab but this still is no gaurantee. Another sure way is to intern during summer. Below are some of the ways to tapeout/explore the process of design.

  • Access to cadence and proprietary software is very important to truly explore analog design that can be taped-out. A good alternative to this is to use open-source tools like Xschem, ngspice, etc.(link)
  • Prof. Harald Pretl has a course on how to use the tools through some great example designs using Gm/Id methodology
  • Gm/Id is an excellent design methodology for short channel technology nodes where traditional equations don't work, Prof. Boris murmann has a book "Systematic Design of Analog CMOS Circuits" on this as well as some short slides
  • Zero to ASIC website sometimes lets you tapeout your own design if it has merit.
  • SSCS has been hosting Chipathon for the past four years where you can tapeout a design for a given theme. I have tried this last year, but couldn't spend enough time for personal reasons, although some people were able to tapeout.
  • You can also showcase your design talent through code-a-chip program
  • SSCS has a student circuit design contest which has attractive rewards like travel grants for conferences.
