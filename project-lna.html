Project Overview

The Low Noise Amplifier (LNA) is the first active component in a wireless receiver chain, and its performance critically impacts the overall system sensitivity. This project focused on designing a high-performance LNA for 2.5 GHz applications, commonly used in WiFi (802.11) and other wireless communication standards.

The LNA must simultaneously achieve low noise figure, sufficient gain, good input matching, high linearity, and low power consumption—often conflicting requirements that demand careful optimization and trade-off analysis.

Design Methodology

Architecture Selection: Common Source with Inductive Degeneration

The common source (CS) topology with inductive source degeneration was selected as it offers several advantages:

  • Simultaneous noise and power matching: The source inductor allows independent optimization of input impedance matching and noise figure
  • Low noise contribution: Inductors are passive and contribute minimal noise compared to resistive degeneration
  • Improved linearity: Source degeneration provides negative feedback, enhancing IIP3
  • Compact layout: Simpler than cascode structures while maintaining good performance

Input Matching Network Design

The input impedance of the LNA is given by:

Zin = sLg + sLs + 1/(sCgs) + gmLs/Cgs

Key design considerations included:

  • Selecting gate inductor (Lg) to resonate with Cgs at 2.5 GHz
  • Choosing source inductor (Ls) to provide real part matching to 50Ω
  • Optimizing transistor width for target gm and Cgs values
  • Minimizing parasitic capacitances through careful layout

Noise Figure Optimization

The noise figure of an LNA with inductive degeneration is dominated by the channel thermal noise of the input transistor. By choosing appropriate bias current and transistor sizing, and using inductive (rather than resistive) degeneration, the noise figure can be minimized while maintaining input matching. The quality factor (Q) of on-chip spiral inductors also plays a critical role.

Load Network and Gain Optimization

The output load network was designed to:

  • Maximize voltage gain through LC resonant tank at 2.5 GHz
  • Provide appropriate output impedance for the following mixer stage
  • Minimize loss from parasitic resistances in inductors
  • Allow easy integration with differential mixer inputs

Key Design Challenges

1. Inductor Design and Layout

On-chip spiral inductors are critical components but suffer from:

  • Low quality factor (Q) due to substrate losses and metal resistance
  • Significant parasitic capacitance to substrate
  • Self-resonance frequency limitations

Solutions implemented:

  • Used top-level thick metal layers to minimize series resistance
  • Implemented patterned ground shield to reduce substrate coupling
  • Optimized inductor geometry (inner diameter, width, spacing) for maximum Q
  • Validated inductor models using EM simulation

2. Stability Analysis

RF amplifiers can be prone to oscillations. Stability was ensured by:

  • Analyzing S-parameters across frequency to verify K-factor > 1
  • Adding source/load stabilization networks if needed
  • Careful PCB/bondwire modeling to account for package parasitics
  • Ensuring unconditional stability from DC to beyond operating frequency

3. Linearity vs. Power Trade-off

Higher bias current improves linearity (IIP3) but increases power consumption. The design balanced:

  • Selecting bias point for optimal gm/ID efficiency
  • Using inductive degeneration to enhance linearity without excessive current
  • Meeting IIP3 specifications for typical wireless blocking scenarios

Simulation Results

The LNA design was extensively simulated in Cadence Spectre with the following performance metrics achieved:

  • Noise Figure: Optimized to be less than 2.5 dB at 2.5 GHz
  • Voltage Gain (S21): Achieved 15-18 dB across the band of interest
  • Input Return Loss (S11): Better than -15 dB, indicating good 50Ω matching
  • Reverse Isolation (S12): Better than -40 dB, ensuring minimal feedback
  • IIP3: Exceeding -10 dBm for acceptable linearity
  • Power Consumption: Less than 15 mW from 1.8V supply

Performance Highlights

The design successfully demonstrated the classic LNA trade-offs: achieving low noise figure while maintaining good input matching, adequate gain, and reasonable power consumption. The inductive degeneration technique proved highly effective in decoupling noise and impedance matching constraints.

Lessons Learned and Future Work

Key insights from this project:

  • The critical importance of accurate inductor modeling at GHz frequencies
  • Trade-offs between NF, gain, linearity, and power are fundamental to LNA design
  • Layout parasitics can significantly impact performance and must be considered early
  • Source degeneration provides elegant solution to simultaneous matching and noise optimization

Potential enhancements for future iterations:

  • Implement cascode topology for improved reverse isolation and higher gain
  • Add multi-band capability through switchable/tunable resonators
  • Explore noise-canceling architectures for improved NF
  • Design for fabrication and measure actual silicon performance
